THESIS PROJECT

EIGHT MONTHS

ENABLING MODULAR LOW COST RECORDING OF SWIMMING

Using a network of inertial sensors, swimmers can record their movement without resorting to bulky multi-camera setups and manual digitalziation. The Double Diamond [1] methodology, divided in divergent and convergent phases, was used. The first phase is Discovery, where a contextual research is made, and is followed by Define, when a design opportunity is pinpointed.

STAKEHOLDER MAPPING

The project started with an immersion into the swimming context, on its professional, amateur and academic spheres. Three major stakeholders/user groups were defined:

discover

#1 SWIMMERS

The primary user group. To get to know this user group, qualitative interviews were conducted with amateur swimmers at a competition.

#2 COACHES

A secondary user group. A training session of professional swimmers was observed, and the coach interviewed.

#2 RESEARCHERS

Another secondary user group. Observation of the process of swimming data collection and analysis was carried to understand the state of the art of the field.

DEFINING THE PROBLEM

The findings of user research, backed by desk research on swimming theory, point to swimming technique as the aspect users would benefit the most from accessing quantitative data and visualization [2], [3]. Therefore, the project objective became: "Provide relevant information about the athletes swimming technique, to enhance his or hers performance".

define

the second diamond

Discover and Define are followed by the divergent Develop phase, where ideation on a concept is made, followed by a convergent Deliver phase where it's scope is settled and it is prepared for shipping or production.

ANALYZING COMPETING PRODUCTS

After defining the problem to be tackled, an analysis of products that tried to accomplish this same task was conducted. The products and systems that could enable users to visualize their swimming technique as well as provide quantitative data on performance could be divided into two main groups:

develop

VIDEOGRAMETRY

Camera-based systems, as the one observed in the user research phase, that film the athletes movement through multiple perspectives and digitalize it into a tridimensional representation, or, if only one camera is used, offers simple playback. Water bubbles and refraction diminish the acuracy of underwater cameras.

INERTIAL SENSORS

Based on miniaturized accelerometer and gyroscopes, also known as MEMS, these are used in fitness trackers to provide metrics on user movement. In one product they were used concatenated, providing full-body motion capture.

IDEATION AND SELECTION

Adding to the similar products research, alternative solutions were brainstormed. The main three alternatives groups could be clustered in:

1) Ultrassound system

A set of ultrassound or similar waves transceivers would be placed on the pool, and another on the swimmers body, to capture the movement. It was not used because the air-water change of medium rendered the time-of-flight of the waves unreliable.

2) Facilitated videogrametry

Inspired on the present state-of-art, a number of alternatives to make it the system easier to assemble or permitting the automation of digitalization were proposed. However, they still relied on the multi-camera setup that was found too difficult to be applied in the first place by all stakeholders but the researchers.

3) Inertial Sensors

A network of inertial sensors, in form of a swimming vest or set of modular straps would record the motion that then could be visualized on a third-party device such as a computer or smartphone, with appropriate software.

EMPLOYING USER RESEARCH TO BACK DESIGN DECISIONS

The user needs that surfaced during research were translated into project requirements, that were then weighted acording to the number of stakeholder groups that had them. This way, the similar products and generated alternatives could be scored as to how closely they attend these needs. The modular wearable of inertial sensors fared the best on the comparison, and thus was taken into further development.

USAGE SCENARIOS

The scope of the project encompassed the specification of a physical product, as well as the logical definition of the system and user experience. To inform the creation of these, three usage scenarios were explored:

1) Single user

A swimmer buys from one to fifteen modules (number used to record the most relevant body segments) to capture his own movement.

2) Solidary users

A group of swimmers buys one module each, to use as fitness trackers, and eventually comes together to record their full body motion using their modules in tandem.

3) Service users

A coach or swimming school buys one or more sets of modules, and use them to record students movement during class.

From these scenarios, a generic, all-encompassing flowchart of the product part of the system was devised. Then, a table of states, actions, errors and solutions was developed in order to shed light into the design solution through an interaction point of view [4].

DEFINING THE PRODUCT SUBSYSTEMS

Informed by the usage flowchart and table, several subsystems of the product were devised and developed.

deliver

SOFTWARE FLOWCHART

Also, a usage flowchart for the product software was created, containing all the actions and responses needed for the system to work. The coding of the software however, was out of the projects scope.

THE RECBOT

The Recbot is a modular system for motion capture comprised of one or more modules to be worn on the body segments: hands, arms, forearms, legs, forelegs, feet, waist, chest and head. Creating an ad hoc Bluetooth low-energy network, the data (acceleration and angular velocity) of all the sensors, recorded internally, can be transferred to a third-party device that processes and concatenates all of it. Thus, it can later be used to animate a 3D rigged character, for movement visualization, or provide metrics on the performance (such as speed, stroke count and stroke lenght).

1) Outside view

The outside of the recbot is modelled to guide the user on the right way to position it on his or her body. Special attention was paid to this point because the modules have to placed in a specific orientation in order for the centralized data to work correctly. See in interactive 3D.

2) Inside view, top

The outer face of the printed circuit board (PCB) inside the Recbot contains the inertial sensor, microcontroller and LED. they were organised this way to minimize the volume and footprint of the board. See in interactive 3D.

3) Inside view, bottom

On the inner face of the circuit board are the microSD card slot that keeps the recorded data locally before Bluetooth transfer, battery, buzzer and switch. See in interactive 3D.

FINAL THOUGHTS

My Bachelor thesis project allowed me to join the overall design process with a deeper understanding of technical development, through a holistic approach.

While the overarching Double Diamond method is employed, other specific methods, such as Garret's Elements of user Experience, and wearable development methodologies were consulted [5]. This way, I tried to ensure the most impactful project for the users, while accounting for the technologic possibilities.
